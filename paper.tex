\begin{abstract}

	Open-source communities have accrued vast infrastructural capital in the form of knowledge, code, trust, and
	shared procedures. This social achievement forms the patchwork of projects upon which modern internet
	infrastructure is built. While corporations have historically extracted value from these commons piecemeal,
	the inherent unstructured nature and the establishment of regulatory spaces have provided resistance to
	total extraction. The advent of \acp{llm} is proving to completely and totally re-shape this
	carefully established ecosystem.
	This work analyzes the intricate interplay between communities and corporations across four pillars:
	\textbf{generation} (the labor of care), \textbf{mediation} (the role of maintainers and regulation),
	\textbf{extraction} (the appropriation of the capital of the commons), and \textbf{depletion} (the crisis of care).
	Drawing parallels to the repurposing of digital infrastructures in targeted advertising, policy-making,
	and ``influence policing'', we argue that \acp{llm} represent a crisis of infrastructural depletion
	that threatens the sustainability of the digital commons.

\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Modern digital society relies on a foundation of invisible scaffolding: the \ac{oss} projects that underpin global infrastructure. 
This section motivates the analysis by framing \ac{oss} not merely as a technical resource, but as a \cite{collier_peering_2025} ``social achievement'' sustained by a delicate balance of community norms and corporate participation. 
We introduce the controversy: the shift from collaborative social shaping to adversarial extraction.

\section{Infrastructure, Capital and Extraction}
To analyze this shift, we propose a framework to define and relate infrastructure, capital and extraction. This model builds upon prior
work, taking in inspiration from the \ac{slti} framework introduced by \citet{stewart_intermediaries_2008}, specifically
building on the concept of intermediaries driving technology adoption and social learning.
As laid out, open-source communities occupy a special role in the \ac{slti} framework, as they are engaging in a form of \emph{co-design}, performing both technology development and diffusion \cite{stewart_intermediaries_2008}.
We want to show that such intermediaries must -- by necessity -- accumulate capital in soft currencies like knowledge and trust
as well as more classical forms like software products and platforms.

We define this as \emph{infrastructural capital}, the accumulated value of a digital commons -- comprising code, shared knowledge, social trust, and procedural norms -- which allows a technical system to function as reliable infrastructure. 
Unlike traditional economic capital, it is not merely a ``resource'' to be spent, but a social achievement maintained through the continuous labor of care and ``peering pressure''.
It is characterized by its social stickiness: its value is embedded in the durable networks of human maintainers and intermediaries who ensure the system's stability, security, and legibility.
This is a natural extension of Bourdieu's definition of capital as accumulated labour \cite{bourdieu_1986}.

We then look at how this infrastructural capital is appropriated (used) by other parties.
Specifically, we want to look at different forms of this appropriation, and differentiate between \emph{collaborative} \cite{collier_peering_2025,skains_creative_2016} and \emph{adversarial} \cite{collier_influence_2024} appropriation.
We define \emph{collaborative appropriation} as the process of copying, transforming, deploying or otherwise adopting infrastructural capital in accordance with the initial goals and values of the entity or entities that create and/or manage this capital.
The definition of \emph{adversarial appropriation} follows from this as appropriation that is not in accordance with such goals and values.

Finally, we investigate \emph{depletion} or \emph{exhaustion} of such capital through over-extraction. 
The depletion of the digital commons is not a technical failure but a \textit{crisis of care} \cite{fraser_contradictions_2016}. As \citet{asparouhova_working_2020} demonstrates, the open-source model is increasingly defined by an asymmetry where a small number of maintainers bear a ``maintenance burden'' that has reached the point of over-exhaustion. 

\section{The Patchwork World of Open Source}
We want to spend some time discussing the development of the open source communities over time, their co-development 
with the wider internet infrastructure and their interactions with the more commercially-interested parties in this system.
We want to focus on intricate interplay of openeness, freedom and commercialisation and how these concepts are shaped and
interpreted across the different open-source communities.

\subsection{A History of Open Infrastructure}
The origins of the internet in scientific collaboration established a precedent for open development and peer-to-peer standard setting. We examine the ideological fracture between \ac{foss} (GPL-focused user freedom) and \ac{oss} (MIT-focused adoption freedom), noting that these licenses are the fragile regulatory frameworks currently under threat.

\subsection{The Capital of the Intermediaries}
\ac{oss} exists as a \textit{patchwork} of varied capital: structured (code), semi-structured (documentation), and unstructured (trust and communal knowledge). Utilizing the \ac{slti} framework \cite{stewart_intermediaries_2008}, we argue that \ac{oss} communities act as tightly-coupled intermediaries that short-circuit traditional innovation cycles, generating a vast ``capital of the commons.'' Historically, corporate extraction (e.g., cloud providers repackaging code) targeted the structured bits, largely leaving the social fabric intact.

\section{The Arrival of LLMs}
We outline the technical shift represented by \acp{llm}, emphasizing their unique ability to ingest and synthesize unstructured inputs.

\subsection{LLMs as Tools of Appropriation}
\acp{llm} represent the ultimate engine for \textit{adversarial appropriation} \cite{collier_influence_2024}. By creating a \cite{collier_influence_2024} ``patchwork profile'' of millions of developers' logic, they perform \textit{license laundering}---effectively converting protected \ac{foss} logic into proprietary outputs. Furthermore, they induce depletion by drowning human intermediaries in AI-generated \acp{pr}, clogging the essential review processes.

\subsection{The Independence Paradox}
While \acp{llm} offer developers increased individual autonomy, we caveat this by arguing that such empowerment often atomizes the community. By reducing the need for human-to-human ties, these tools may inadvertently weaken the social network that generates the very capital the models rely upon.

\section{Conclusion: Seizing the Capital of the Commons}
The \ac{oss} community has been invaluable in creating the infrastructure of the modern age, generating immense infrastructural capital through a culture of care \cite{collier_peering_2025}. While a tenuous balance between communities and corporations was maintained for decades, the advent of \acp{llm} has fundamentally upended this equilibrium.

The controversy is not merely a legal dispute over copyright, but a sociological crisis of \textbf{infrastructural sustainability}. When we automate the extraction of logic but leave the burden of care to overstretched humans, we risk the irreversible destruction of the digital commons. The crisis of care in \ac{oss} serves as a warning for a data society that risks strip-mining its own social foundations.
